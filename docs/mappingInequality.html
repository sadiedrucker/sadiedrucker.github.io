Mapping Inequality in Los Angeles, California

Guiding Research Questions

  1. Are neighborhoods segregated by race and/or socioeconomic status?
  2. Is poverty spatially concentrated?
  3. How does public transportation connect different neighborhoods?

Mathematical Tools

I employed methods such as Graph Theory and Counting to develop a model that could address these research questions.

Graph Theory

I aimed to create a graph of neighborhoods in Los Angeles where nodes represented neighborhoods and edges were established between nodes if public transportation between them takes less than thirty minutes. I then overlaid poverty and race data on the graph.

Counting

There are 114 officially recognized neighborhoods in Los Angeles.

If I wanted to keep track of the public transportation travel time between every pair of neighborhoods, I would need to look up the travel time for every possible origin–destination combination.

We consider the set of \(V\) of \(n\), where \(n=114\) neighborhoods

We want to count the number of ordered pairs \((u,v)\) such that:

  • \(u \in V\) (origin)

  • \(v \in V\) (destination)\

  • \(u \ne v\) (no self loops)

The total number of such pairs is:\(=P(114, 2)=\frac{114!}{(114-2)!}=\frac{114!}{112!}=n(n-1)=114*113=12882\)

This means there are 12,882 unique direction queries to collect.

Looking up this many connections manually would be impossible in practice.

Once I recognized the scale of the problem, I designed an automated system using the Google Directions API to generate the full set of travel-time connections programmatically.

Conclusions

  1. Strong correlation between the percent of non-white residents and poverty.

  2. Poorest neighborhoods are the most connected (nodes with the highest degrees).

  3. Impoverished neighborhoods are primarily connected to other impoverished neighborhoods (and similarly, wealthy neighborhoods are connected to other wealthy neighborhoods).

Limitations

Firstly, transportation data does not represent an average across all days and times of day. The data was collected at 11 AM on a Saturday because that is when the code was run. Similarly, the directions were taken from an arbitrarily determined “center” of each neighborhood by Google Directions.

There is also an inconsistency between the counting methods and the actual graph. When counting (Figure 1), each ordered pair was considered separately, i.e., \((v, u) \neq (u, v)\), but this distinction is not represented in the graph (Figure 3), where edges are treated as undirected.

Finally, in simplifying the data for the final visualization, I chose to group all non-white racial categories into a single category to highlight neighborhood diversity. In doing this, I acknowledge that I combined distinct cultures, identities, and heritages into a single label of “diversity.” This risks oversimplifying and diminishing the uniqueness of the communities represented. In the future, I could represent racial demographics with more nuance and specificity.
